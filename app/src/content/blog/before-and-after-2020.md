title: "Graduate Salaries in Singapore: Before and After 2020"
description: "Splitting the Graduate Employment Survey into two six-year windows — 2014-2019 and 2020-2025 — reveals a clean break. Almost every degree pays more now. Which ones actually grew in real terms is a different question."
pubDate: 2025-04-10
author: "GES Explorer"
---

2020 is a natural dividing line in the Graduate Employment Survey data. It splits 12 years of Singapore salaries into two equal windows: six years before the pandemic, six years after. On one side: a flat, predictable decade. On the other: COVID, a tech hiring boom, and Singapore's biggest inflation spike in a generation. The numbers look very different depending on which side of 2020 you stand on.

## The overall picture

For the 122 degrees where we have usable data in both windows, the pattern is clean.

| Period | Average median salary |
|---|---|
| 2014-2019 | $3,486 |
| 2020-2025 | $4,152 |

That is a 19.5% jump at the median. The average degree pays about $666 more per month after 2020 than it did before. Almost every individual degree moved up — out of the 122 tracked, only a handful stayed flat or fell behind.

Spread it across six years and the pre-2020 window looks almost stagnant in comparison. Between 2013 and 2019, the median degree grew about 17%. Between 2019 and 2025, it grew 23-24%. The post-2020 period produced roughly 40% more nominal salary growth than the comparable stretch before it.

## Who won

The top of the table is dominated by tech and business. These are the ten biggest pre-to-post-2020 jumps across all six universities.

| University | Degree | Pre-2020 avg | Post-2020 avg | Change |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| NUS | Bachelor of Computing (Computer Science) | $4,134 | $6,179 | +49% |
| NUS | Bachelor of Business Administration | $3,287 | $4,869 | +48% |
| NUS | Bachelor of Engineering (Computer Engineering) | $3,879 | $5,565 | +43% |
| NTU | Computer Science | $3,675 | $5,133 | +40% |
| NTU | Computer Engineering | $3,629 | $4,990 | +38% |
| NUS | Bachelor of Computing (Information Systems) | $3,956 | $5,416 | +37% |
| NUS | Bachelor of Arts (Industrial Design) | $3,005 | $3,983 | +33% |
| SIT | Computing Science (with Honours) | $3,740 | $4,920 | +32% |
| NTU | Information Engineering and Media | $3,545 | $4,633 | +31% |
| NUS | Bachelor of Engineering (Electrical Engineering) | $3,566 | $4,641 | +30% |

Nothing surprising about the winners. What is surprising is how far they pulled ahead. NUS Computer Science averaged about $4,100 a month in the pre-2020 window and nearly $6,200 after. That is a $2,000 monthly swing in six years. NUS Business Administration graduates went from roughly $3,300 to almost $4,900.

## Who stayed flat

The other end of the table is more interesting.

| University | Degree | Pre-2020 avg | Post-2020 avg | Change |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| NTU | Medicine | $5,700 | $5,712 | +0% |
| SUTD | Bachelor of Science (Architecture and Sustainable Design) | $3,950 | $4,066 | +3% |
| SIT | Diagnostic Radiography | $3,499 | $3,650 | +4% |
| SIT | Occupational Therapy | $3,268 | $3,400 | +4% |
| SIT | Mechanical Engineering | $3,316 | $3,500 | +6% |
| NUS | Bachelor of Science (Pharmacy) | $3,666 | $3,871 | +6% |
| SIT | Interior Design (with Honours) | $2,648 | $2,800 | +6% |
| SIT | Communication Design (with Honours) | $2,716 | $2,900 | +7% |
| SIT | Nursing (with Honours) | $3,650 | $3,902 | +7% |

NTU Medicine is the standout: essentially zero growth in nominal terms. Starting salaries for housemen in Singapore are set through a national framework, so it reflects policy rather than market forces.

The rest of the list is instructive. Nearly every flat performer is in healthcare, design, or construction-adjacent fields. These are sectors where entry-level pay is tightly anchored — by public-sector pay grids, by regulated training pipelines, or by stagnant hiring demand. Tech and business went up on a rising market. Healthcare and design mostly went sideways.

## The inflation problem

A 19.5% nominal raise over six years is not as generous as it sounds, but it is not negligible either.

Singapore's Consumer Price Index rose roughly 11% between the midpoint of the pre-2020 window (around 2016-17) and the midpoint of the post-2020 window (around 2022-23). Most of that came in a single 18-month spike: 2022 alone was +6.1%, 2023 was +4.8%. The mid-2010s averaged closer to flat. (Source: [SingStat CPI series via data.gov.sg](https://data.gov.sg/datasets/d_bdaff844e3ef89d39fceb962ff8f0791/view).)

That means the average degree's real purchasing power grew about 8% over the six-year window — roughly 1.3% per year in real terms. Modest, but real. The typical graduate is genuinely better off, just not by anywhere near the headline 19.5%.

Redoing the two tables above in real terms still tells two very different stories. The winners still won comfortably: NUS Computing, NTU Computer Science, and NUS Business Administration all cleared inflation by 20-30 percentage points. Many of the "flat" performers, on the other hand, took real pay cuts. NTU Medicine's 0% nominal change is a real cut of about 11%. SIT's healthcare and design programmes, growing 4-7% nominally, lost ground in real terms too.

## University by university

Average nominal growth 2014-2019 to 2020-2025, by institution:

| University | Degrees tracked | Mean growth | Median growth |
|---|---|---|---|
| NUS | 36 | 22.7% | 21.4% |
| SMU | 7 | 21.4% | 22.3% |
| NTU | 37 | 20.2% | 19.8% |
| SUSS | 4 | 18.0% | 17.0% |
| SIT | 34 | 14.8% | 14.7% |
| SUTD | 4 | 12.2% | 12.3% |

NUS, SMU, and NTU cluster in the 20-23% range — broadly similar, probably because they have similar mixes of tech, business, and traditional academic degrees. SIT is a full seven percentage points behind. That makes sense: SIT specializes in applied, practice-linked degrees (nursing, radiography, occupational therapy, various design and engineering trades), and those are exactly the fields that tracked flat. SUTD's four tracked degrees are similarly anchored, though with only four data points it is hard to say much with confidence.

## What it means if you are choosing a degree now

Two uncomfortable conclusions.

**First, the tech premium has compounded into something real.** At the start of the 2010s, a computing graduate made roughly the same starting salary as a humanities graduate. Today the gap is often $1,500-2,000 a month. That is not a reflection of a single hot year — it is the accumulated result of a decade of different growth rates.

**Second, nominal salary growth is not the same as getting ahead.** If you pick a degree because its nominal starting salary has been climbing, you may be picking a degree that is barely keeping up with inflation. Healthcare, architecture, and several design fields have seen modest nominal raises that translate to real wage cuts. This is not a judgment on whether those fields are worth studying — it is a reminder that the sticker price on a degree is not the whole story.

The 2020-2025 period is probably not representative of the next six years. Tech hiring has cooled. Singapore's inflation has come back down. The specific winners and losers on this list may look different by the time the 2030 data comes in. But the divergence itself — between degrees with market-linked pay and degrees with policy-linked pay — is likely to keep widening.
