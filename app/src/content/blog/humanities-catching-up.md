For most of the 2010s, studying humanities was a quiet kind of gamble. Median starting salaries for English, Chinese, and Sociology graduates barely moved from year to year. In some cases they actually went down. The same pattern held at both NTU's College of Humanities, Arts & Social Sciences and NUS's Faculty of Arts & Social Sciences. Meanwhile computing and engineering majors watched their starting offers climb steadily.

Then 2019 happened. Then COVID. Then inflation. And something interesting started showing up in the numbers.

## The lost decade

Here is what happened to humanities majors at NTU and NUS between 2013 and 2019, the six years before the pandemic.

| University | Degree | 2013 median | 2019 median | Change |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| NTU | English | $3,100 | $3,000 | -3% |
| NTU | Sociology | $3,100 | $3,300 | +6% |
| NTU | Chinese | $3,000 | $3,210 | +7% |
| NTU | Communication Studies | $3,000 | $3,200 | +7% |
| NUS | Bachelor of Arts (Hons) | $3,200 | $3,500 | +9% |
| NUS | Bachelor of Social Sciences | $3,200 | $3,500 | +9% |
| NTU | Psychology | $3,000 | $3,400 | +13% |
| NTU | Linguistics and Multilingual Studies | $2,625 | $3,200 | +22% |
| NTU | Art, Design and Media | $2,500 | $3,000 | +20% |

Six years. NTU English graduates were earning less in nominal dollars in 2019 than they were in 2013. NTU Sociology grew at 1% per year. NUS Bachelor of Arts (Hons) and Bachelor of Social Sciences grew about 9% over the whole period — less than 1.5% per year. Even after a decade of compounding, most degrees still started under $3,500.

## The comeback

Now look at the same degrees from 2019 to 2025.

| University | Degree | 2019 median | 2025 median | Change |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| NTU | English | $3,000 | $4,100 | +37% |
| NTU | Sociology | $3,300 | $4,500 | +36% |
| NTU | Chinese | $3,210 | $4,345 | +35% |
| NUS | Bachelor of Arts (Hons) | $3,500 | $4,680 | +34% |
| NUS | Bachelor of Social Sciences | $3,500 | $4,500 | +29% |
| NTU | Communication Studies | $3,200 | $4,000 | +25% |
| NTU | Linguistics and Multilingual Studies | $3,200 | $3,950 | +23% |
| NTU | Psychology | $3,400 | $4,065 | +20% |
| NTU | Art, Design and Media | $3,000 | $3,350 | +12% |

NTU English flipped from the worst performer to one of the best, growing 37% in six years. NUS Bachelor of Arts (Hons) moved 34% over the same period after essentially stagnating in the prior decade. Sociology, Chinese, Communication Studies, and NUS Social Sciences all moved 25-36%. These are growth rates that look like computing degrees from the 2010s — and the pattern is nearly identical at both NTU and NUS, which suggests this is not an institution-specific story.

To put it another way: NTU Computer Science grew 31% over the same 2019-2025 period. NTU Mechanical Engineering grew 25%. The "soft" degrees were keeping pace with the "hard" ones.

## The catch

Two things complicate the comeback story.

**First, inflation.** Singapore's consumer price index rose roughly 17-18% between 2019 and 2025, almost all of it concentrated in the 2022-2023 spike (+6.1% and +4.8% respectively, per [SingStat](https://data.gov.sg/datasets/d_bdaff844e3ef89d39fceb962ff8f0791/view)). A 37% nominal raise is closer to a 16% real raise over six years, which is still a genuine improvement — just less dramatic than the headline number.

**Second, the absolute gap to computing kept widening.** Percentage growth tells one story; dollar gaps tell another. Here is the gap between NTU Computer Science and four humanities degrees, in 2013 and in 2025.

| Degree | Gap to CS in 2013 | Gap to CS in 2025 |
|---|---|---|
| Sociology | $25 | $1,000 |
| Chinese | $125 | $1,155 |
| English | $25 | $1,400 |
| Psychology | $125 | $1,435 |

In 2013, an NTU Sociology graduate started $25 a month behind a Computer Science graduate. In 2025, that gap is $1,000 a month — $12,000 a year before any raises. The percentage growth is similar in the recent period, but the dollar premium for picking up a CS degree has compounded into something significant.

## What it might mean

Some possibilities, none of which we can prove from this dataset alone.

- **A wage floor effect.** Singapore's progressive wage moves and minimum wage discussions during COVID may have lifted entry-level salaries for non-technical graduates more than the top end.
- **A demand shift.** The post-COVID economy needed more communicators, designers, and policy thinkers, not fewer. Companies that under-hired humanities grads in the 2010s may have caught up.
- **A normalization.** The 2013-2019 stagnation may have been the anomaly, not the recent growth. Perhaps humanities salaries were artificially depressed during the tech-dominated decade and are now rebounding to a more reasonable level.
- **Survey timing.** Each year's survey is taken six months after graduation. Cohorts that graduated in 2019-2020 hit a uniquely cold job market, which depresses the baseline against which we are measuring.

The story you choose probably says more about your priors than about the data. What the numbers show is straightforward: after a decade of stagnation, humanities graduates at NTU are seeing meaningful nominal raises. The premium for choosing a STEM degree is still very real, and probably bigger in dollar terms than it has ever been. Whether the recent growth reflects a permanent rebalancing or a one-off catch-up will take a few more years of data to know.

In the meantime, if you are an English, History, or Social Sciences major at either NTU or NUS, your starting salary just grew faster than it has in any six-year stretch on record.
